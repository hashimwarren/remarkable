# Article Routes

Choose the article's invisible persuasive architecture after framework work and before creating the working outline. The writer chooses between two concrete routes, not between copywriting acronyms.

## Assemble the rhetorical brief

Use the governing premise, reader, current belief, desired movement, why-now context, confirmed objection and response direction, approved Personal Authority when present, approved practical framework when present, project context, and known truth boundaries. Do not ask the writer to repeat established material.

Privately make the best-supported guess about the reader's current awareness: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, or most aware. Use that judgment only to decide where the article must begin and what it may responsibly assume. Do not expose the label or add a questionnaire. Ask one focused follow-up only when uncertainty would produce fundamentally different articles.

## Develop candidate routes

Use recognized structures as compact model-facing vocabulary:

- **PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve**
- **AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action**
- **4Ps — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push**
- **HSO — Hook, Story, Offer**
- **FAB — Features, Advantages, Benefits**
- **ACCA — Awareness, Comprehension, Conviction, Action**

These are starting points, not an exhaustive taxonomy or topic-to-template mapping. Adapt or combine them, or use another coherent persuasive architecture when it better serves the premise. Do not rotate structures, consult prior-article history, or run a separate sameness check.

For every serious candidate, determine privately:

- where this reader begins;
- the sequence of beliefs or realizations;
- what creates forward momentum;
- where Personal Authority belongs;
- where the objection is answered;
- where the approved framework or answer is revealed;
- what proof the route requires; and
- how the ending completes the premise.

Reject a candidate that merely renames another route, requires unsupported claims, delays the premise without purpose, forces the story or framework into the article, or lacks a satisfying destination. Select the two routes most likely to change this reader's mind while remaining truthful, coherent, and supportable.

## Advocate two routes

Keep this checkpoint brief and energetic:

> We have the premise, the reader's objection, and the material that can support it. I see two strong ways to make the case. Choose one, and I'll turn it into the working outline.

Present exactly:

```markdown
### A. [Concrete route name]
[Two or three vivid sentences explaining how the article would unfold and why this route gives the premise persuasive force.]

### B. [Concrete route name]
[Two or three vivid sentences explaining how the article would unfold and why this route gives the premise persuasive force.]
```

Advocate for both routes. Name the specific source of momentum, credibility, surprise, clarity, or proof that makes each promising. Do not expose awareness labels, framework acronyms, or internal taxonomy. The two routes must describe meaningfully different reading experiences, not alternate headlines on the same sequence.

Ask exactly:

> Which direction should govern the outline: A or B?

Use structured input when available:

- **A — [short route name]**
- **B — [short route name]**

Use the same plain-text fallback. STOP and wait. Accept selection by letter, route name, or unambiguous natural language. The free-form response may request a combination, revision, or another direction. When it materially changes the route, present and reconfirm the revised route before outlining.

## Carry the selection forward

After selection, confirm the route in one sentence and preserve a compact private route brief containing the starting point, belief sequence, objection placement, Personal Authority placement, framework or answer reveal, proof needs, and ending movement. Do not create a separate route artifact or write the architecture label to `PREMISE.md`.

Create the working outline directly from this brief. The architecture should shape headings and section movement without turning the outline into a visible fill-in-the-blanks formula.

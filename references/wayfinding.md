# Workflow Wayfinding

Keep the writer oriented without turning the workflow into another document to read.

## Mark meaningful transitions

At the beginning of each major stage, after returning from Roughdraft, and when entering a revision loop, provide:

1. one sentence naming what the writer is deciding or producing now and why it matters;
2. one sentence naming what happens immediately afterward; and
3. a compact stage marker.

Do not repeat this before every question. Keep the two sentences under 45 words total.

Use this Markdown-safe marker and update it truthfully:

```text
Remarkable  ● Premise → ● Objection → ◉ Story → ○ Framework → ○ Route → ○ Outline → ○ Proof → ○ Approval → ○ Draft → ○ Slopless → ○ Critique
```

- `●` completed
- `◉` current
- `○` upcoming
- `–` skipped optional stage

Do not show percentages or time estimates. Branching and revision make them misleading. When useful, state the number of short decisions remaining before the next artifact.

## Make earned progress visible

After a consequential confirmation, state the value now secured and name the next stage in one compact sentence. Do not congratulate routine button presses or use game-like points, streaks, badges, or exaggerated celebration. Distinguish required decisions from optional improvements.

## Close without a dead end

At a natural completion point, say:

> This version is ready for you to use.

Name all four available continuations in chat:

- **Run another Remarkable critique**
- **Strengthen one section**
- **Help produce the proof or visuals**
- **Prepare the final publishable version**

Codex structured input supports at most three buttons, so use:

- **Run another critique**
- **Strengthen one section**
- **Finish or produce assets** — the writer can specify proof, visuals, or publication in the free-form response.

Use the four-item list as the plain-text fallback. Always include another critique, but never imply that it is required. Give a repeated critique a useful lens when context supports one: fresh-reader, skeptical-reader, or final pre-publication. Do not manufacture another revision when the article is ready.

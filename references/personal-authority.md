# Personal Authority

Personal Authority connects the governing premise to an experience that makes the argument credibly and memorably the writer's. It is rhetorical material, not decorative autobiography or a substitute for evidence.

Use this stage only after the premise is confirmed. If the writer has no relevant experience or does not want to use one, skip it without pressure and do not create an empty artifact section.

## Discover the experience

Ask exactly:

> Is there something you discovered, struggled through, or changed your mind about that led you to this premise?

Accept fragments and conversational answers. Recover only facts needed to understand:

- what the writer previously believed or expected;
- the concrete situation or contradiction they encountered;
- what they tried, lost, learned, or changed;
- the turning point;
- how the experience led to the premise; and
- why sharing it helps the reader.

Do not ask every question mechanically. Ask only what is missing and consequential. Never invent scenes, dialogue, motives, chronology, emotional reactions, requests to teach, or results.

## Select the approach internally

Choose the approach that best fits the writer's supplied experience. Do not make the writer learn or select the taxonomy.

### Earned Discovery

Frank Kern's Reluctant Hero pattern. Establish credibility and relationship without bragging:

1. The writer was pursuing ordinary work rather than seeking authority.
2. They encountered or discovered a consequential method, mechanism, or insight.
3. They applied it and observed bounded results.
4. Other people asked for help or teaching, when that actually happened.
5. They chose to share it because it can help the reader.

Never fabricate reluctance, acclaim, demand, results, or people asking to be taught. Authority must be earned through the facts, not asserted through a marketing trope.

### Shared Struggle

Frank Kern's Us vs. Them pattern. Create solidarity, reveal an obscured mechanism, and invite action around shared values:

1. The writer discovers something important.
2. A powerful actor, system, convention, or incentive benefits from it remaining obscure.
3. The discovery changes how responsibility for the problem should be understood.
4. The writer reveals it to advance a shared goal rather than merely elevate themselves.
5. Taking action expresses membership in a group with shared values.
6. Inaction preserves the condition being challenged.

The opposing force may be a powerful company, institution, business model, incentive, convention, policy, or belief when its role can be supported. It must never be a specific person—public or private—or a vulnerable group. Criticize consequential choices and systems precisely. Do not invent motives, conspiracies, concealment, or collective guilt.

Use “secret” or “they don't want you to know” only when evidence supports actual concealment. Otherwise describe the mechanism accurately as overlooked, structurally hidden, counterintuitive, poorly explained, or visible mainly from an inside position.

### Transformation

Frank Kern's Hometown Boy Makes Good pattern. Use struggle, loss, change, or redemption to make movement emotionally credible:

1. Establish the writer's earlier condition or belief.
2. Show the loss, failure, tension, or constraint that made change necessary.
3. Identify the choices and difficulty involved in changing.
4. Show the turning point without smoothing away uncertainty.
5. Connect the resulting change to the governing premise.
6. Make the writer's movement relevant to the movement asked of the reader.

Do not force a dramatic fall-and-redemption arc onto gradual learning or ordinary experience.

## Read back the story architecture

Do not draft the article passage yet. Read back a compact factual structure:

```markdown
**Personal Authority — [Earned Discovery, Shared Struggle, or Transformation]**

You originally believed [prior belief]. While [specific situation], you encountered [problem or contradiction]. After [struggle, experiment, discovery, or change], you realized [turning point]. That experience led you to believe [connection to the premise]. You are sharing it because [reader-facing purpose].

Does this accurately represent what happened and why it led you to the premise?
```

Adapt the language to the actual approach rather than forcing every sentence. Revise until the writer explicitly approves it. Let the writer correct details, provide more context, reduce personal exposure, or skip the story.

## Preserve the approved architecture

Append a lean section to `PREMISE.md` only after approval:

```markdown
## Personal Authority

Approach: [Earned Discovery, Shared Struggle, or Transformation]

**Approved story:**
[The approved factual story architecture connecting the writer's experience to the premise.]

[For Shared Struggle, when relevant:]
**Opposing force:**
[The supported system, company, incentive, convention, policy, or belief.]

**Shared identity:**
[The relevant values or goal connecting writer and reader.]
```

Omit the bracketed Shared Struggle fields for Earned Discovery and Transformation, and when they are irrelevant to a particular Shared Struggle account. Do not add recommended placement, truth-boundary, privacy-boundary, headline, or polished article prose. If the writer skips Personal Authority, omit the entire section.

# Framework Design

The premise governs what the reader should believe. A framework may package what the reader should do next. Do not force every article to have one.

## Detect a genuine opportunity

After the article map is resolved and before the working outline, privately inspect the writer's advice for:

- several elements that work together;
- an order of operations or maturity path;
- recurring choices with different answers;
- inputs that must be weighed;
- distinct situations requiring different treatment; or
- conditional actions.

Continue directly to the outline without mentioning frameworks when none would make the advice easier to understand, remember, apply, or transmit. Do not create a checkpoint merely to report that no opportunity exists.

When a genuine opportunity exists, describe the concrete benefit rather than naming a framework taxonomy. Then use structured input when available:

- **Develop the framework**
- **Show me the possibility**
- **Keep it as prose**

Use the same choices as a plain-text fallback. **Develop** and **Show** both lead to a concrete proposed structure before anything enters the article. **Keep it as prose** skips framework work without weakening the premise or advice.

## Tier 1: operational structures

Choose the best-fitting structure internally. Do not ask the writer to select an abstract type.

### Whole / Parts / Whole

Shows a complete system, its interacting components, and how the parts recombine. Use when readers need both the whole model and the role of each part.

### Formula

Organizes inputs, weights, scores, or relationships used to evaluate an outcome. Use only when the underlying judgment supports explicit comparison or calculation; never imply false precision.

### Progression

Organizes advice into ordered steps, stages, or levels. Use when sequence, maturity, dependency, or cumulative development genuinely matters.

### Categories

Separates related situations into meaningful groups or dimensions. Use when readers are treating unlike situations as though they were the same. A matrix is optional, not the default.

### Decision Tree

Organizes conditional choices. Use when the correct action changes according to the reader's circumstances, inputs, or previous answers.

## Tier 2: memorable expression

These techniques may strengthen approved advice or an operational framework, but they are not substitutes for structure:

- **Maxim:** compress a sound principle into an accurate, memorable statement.
- **Naming:** give an established mechanism or framework distinctive language.
- **Allegory:** explain an abstract structure through a memorable story or analogy.
- **Exemplar:** use a recognizable example or archetype to embody relevant attributes.

Do not run a separate Tier 2 checkpoint in this release. Use a Tier 2 technique only when it clarifies a sound structure without exaggerating novelty. A catchy name cannot rescue weak advice.

## Develop and confirm

1. Select the most honest Tier 1 structure.
2. Organize only the writer's existing advice and supported implications.
3. Give it a provisional descriptive title, not a proprietary-sounding brand name by default.
4. Show its governing logic, components or branches, one realistic example, and important limit.
5. Explain in one sentence why this structure fits.
6. Use structured input when available:
   - **Develop it**
   - **Revise the logic**
   - **Try another structure**
7. Use the same choices as a plain-text fallback. Accept free-form requests to keep it as prose.
8. Revise until the writer explicitly approves it or returns to prose.

Never interpret enthusiasm, silence, or a request to see the possibility as approval. Do not add a proposed framework to an artifact until it is explicitly approved.

## Persist it in the article map

Add an approved framework to the existing article map, not `PREMISE.md` and not a separate framework artifact:

```markdown
## Practical framework: [Provisional descriptive title]

*Turn the proven argument into a reusable tool. Establish the framework's governing logic before presenting its components.*

- Governing logic: [Why these components, variables, stages, categories, or conditions belong together.]
- Structure: [The actual parts, calculation, progression, categories, or branches.]
- Demonstration: [A realistic example that shows how to use it.]
- Limit: [Where it does not apply or what judgment it cannot replace.]
- Proof or author input still needed: [Specific gap, or none.]

[FRAMEWORK VISUAL PLACEHOLDER: recommended form and its comprehension job]
```

Adapt the fields to the chosen structure rather than filling irrelevant bullets. The approved framework then flows into the working outline and draft.

## Reject framework-shaped slop

Reject or revise a proposal that:

- converts ordinary bullets into an acronym;
- invents arbitrary steps, categories, weights, or branches;
- renames a familiar idea without adding useful structure;
- oversimplifies consequential judgment;
- implies precision unsupported by evidence;
- creates a visual without improving a decision or explanation; or
- cannot be demonstrated with a realistic example.

A framework does not replace proof, objection handling, limitations, or the writer's judgment. It bridges a proven argument to useful action.

## Carry its visual logic forward

Record the natural visual form as a recommendation for `visual-placeholders.md`:

- Whole / Parts / Whole → system or component map
- Formula → equation, scorecard, or weighted model
- Progression → sequence, staircase, path, or maturity model
- Categories → grouped map or matrix
- Decision Tree → flowchart

The visual must reveal the framework's intellectual structure rather than decorate it. It may occupy one of the outline's two in-article visual opportunities when that is the strongest use.

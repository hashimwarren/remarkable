# Question and Resolution

Use question-and-resolution design internally to give a persuasive article forward motion. Do not teach the writer terms such as “open loop,” add a new workflow checkpoint, or force every article into a mystery.

## Derive the article question

After framework work is approved or skipped and before creating the article map, privately inspect:

- the governing premise;
- the approved Personal Authority story when present;
- the confirmed objection and response direction;
- the approved practical framework when present; and
- the writer's supported facts and examples.

Look for a consequential contrast, contradiction, surprising outcome, failed expectation, or unexplained change that the premise can resolve. Reduce it to:

```markdown
Article question: [What the reader should genuinely want to understand.]
Answer: [The specific insight that resolves that exact question.]
Reveal: [The section or movement where the answer should become clear.]
Framework role: [Answer, embodiment, application, or none.]
```

Do not persist this block in `PREMISE.md`. Carry its substance into the article map and working outline in ordinary reader-facing language. When no genuine question emerges, use a direct tension-and-resolution structure and remove question-specific markers rather than manufacturing curiosity.

## Choose where the question emerges

Place the question where it creates the strongest honest forward pressure:

- in the first sentence when the contrast is immediately intelligible;
- after a Personal Authority scene when the reader needs the situation first; or
- after the problem and stakes when the question would otherwise be abstract.

The question may be explicit or implicit. Give the reader enough context to understand why it matters, but do not state the complete answer before its planned reveal. Curiosity cannot depend on concealing basic facts the reader needs to interpret the situation.

## Build the path to the answer

Before the reveal, each major movement should do at least one useful job:

- deepen the question;
- raise its stakes;
- eliminate an incomplete or obvious explanation; or
- narrow what a satisfying answer must explain.

At the reveal, answer the same question the article raised. After the reveal, explain, prove, demonstrate, qualify, or apply the answer. Do not keep withholding once the article has earned the insight.

Use headings and outline bullets to make this progression visible without naming the technique. Prefer concrete headings that establish the situation, test an explanation, reveal the mechanism, or apply the answer over generic labels such as “Background,” “Problem,” and “Solution.”

## Test the resolution

A satisfying resolution is:

- **Exact:** it answers the question actually raised rather than switching topics.
- **Specific:** it identifies a mechanism, distinction, decision, or process rather than offering a slogan.
- **Earned:** the story, argument, and proof make the answer credible.
- **Proportionate:** the strength of the answer matches the evidence.
- **Consequential:** accepting it changes what the reader understands, decides, or does.
- **Completed:** the article returns to the original situation and shows what the answer now means.

Reject bait-and-switch questions, artificial suspense, inflated mystery, and resolutions that merely announce a framework without showing why it explains the situation.

## Assign the framework's role

An approved practical framework can serve one of three roles:

1. **Answer:** its structure directly explains the unresolved situation.
2. **Embodiment:** an insight or diagnosis answers the question, and the framework makes that answer concrete and transferable.
3. **Application:** the answer arrives first, and the framework helps the reader act on it.

Do not delay the answer until the framework merely because a framework exists. When no framework belongs, place the answer in the most fitting later body movement and prove or demonstrate it there.
